# Voice of Customer: Leasehold / Repairs / Service Charge Assistant

**Document status:** Draft v2
**Purpose:** Captures public voice-of-customer signals showing that leaseholders experience recurring pain around managing agents, service charges, repairs, unclear invoices, poor communication, and overloaded resident directors.

The goal is not to prove legal conclusions. The goal is to show that the underlying problem is real, repeated, emotionally charged, operationally messy, and structured enough to be a candidate for automation.

---

## Executive summary

Across public discussions, the same pattern appears again and again:
- Service charges rise sharply and are hard to understand or challenge
- Managing agents are slow, vague, or unresponsive
- Residents do not trust whether billed work actually happened
- Repairs can drag on for months or years
- Volunteer resident directors are overloaded and under-informed
- The consequences are financial stress, social tension inside the building, and sometimes an inability to sell the flat

This is not just a "complaining online" category. It is a recurring, document-heavy, multi-party coordination problem with high emotional and financial stakes.

## Theme 4: Billed work vs. resident reality

This is the strongest proof point for the proposed product design. It shows exactly why a building-chat bot that gathers corrections from residents is useful.

> "They used money from our fees to pay for work that had never been completed." — Reddit / LegalAdviceUK / 2024

> "The management company still invoiced for their percentage fee despite the work not going forward." — Reddit / HousingUK / 2026

> "The management company collected money… This work has not yet been started… no update… for about 6 months." — Reddit / HousingUK / 2026

> "the first anyone else knew about the works was when the scaffolding started" — MoneySavingExpert / 2023

**Product implication:** The product should convert resident messages into structured contradiction handling:
- What the managing agent claims
- Who disputes it
- What evidence exists
- What proof is still missing
- What clarification should be requested next

## Pattern summary

Taken together, these discussions point to a recurring 7-step workflow:

1. A letter, invoice, estimate, or update arrives
2. Residents do not fully understand it
3. Someone suspects something is wrong, missing, or inflated
4. The group has to ask the relevant flats what really happened
5. Old documents need to be found and compared
6. The managing agent has to be chased or challenged
7. Someone has to decide and approve the next formal move

This is exactly the kind of repeated, messy, high-stakes process that a building AI copilot can productively automate.

## Why this matters for product strategy

This category is attractive because it is:
- Recurring
- Document-heavy
- Multi-party
- Emotionally loaded
- Financially meaningful
- Painful in ways that are visible and quotable in public forums

It is also structurally well suited for an AI copilot because the work is made of:
- Reading and explaining documents
- Collecting missing information
- Surfacing contradictions
- Finding relevant prior records
- Tracking unanswered questions
- Drafting the next formal step

---

## Suggested positioning

The opportunity is not "an AI chatbot for leaseholders."

The opportunity is:

> **A shared AI evidence and escalation manager for leaseholders and resident directors that helps them understand charges, verify whether billed work actually happened, gather resident corrections, compare new claims with old records, and prepare the next approved response.**

---

## Buyer signal: who pays and why

Based on VoC analysis, the **resident director** is the most likely first buyer because:

1. They feel the coordination burden most acutely
2. They are personally exposed if the building gets the challenge wrong
3. They would pay to reduce time, chaos, and mistake risk
4. They control onboarding of other residents

Willingness to pay is highest when the product can demonstrably:
- Save money (challenging an inflated charge)
- Reduce risk (catching a missing document before a tribunal)
- Reduce time (turning a 2-hour admin task into a 10-minute review)

---

## Reusable lesson for future household copilots

The best automation opportunities in public consumer pain are usually the ones where:
- People complain in repeated patterns across forums and years
- Multiple parties are involved
- Documents or screenshots are central
- The current workaround is manual coordination
- The user keeps asking the same questions in slightly different cases
- The cost of confusion is high enough that better organization creates real value

This leasehold case is a strong example of that pattern.
